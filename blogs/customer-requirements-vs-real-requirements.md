Here's a hard pill to swallow: your customers are lying to you. Not maliciously, not on purpose, but they lie all the same. They tell you your idea is brilliant, they say they'd "definitely pay for it", they nod enthusiastically during your demo. Then you build the thing for six months, launch it, and... crickets. Nobody buys. What went wrong?

This is the exact problem Rob Fitzpatrick tackles in *The Mom Test*. The title comes from a brutally honest observation — if you pitch your startup idea to your mom, she'll tell you it's amazing. Because she loves you. Because she wants to encourage you. Not because your idea is actually good. And the painful truth is that most customers treat you with a similar (if less maternal) courtesy. So how do you get to the truth?

### The Three Rules of The Mom Test

Fitzpatrick boils it down to three deceptively simple rules. Stick to these and you'll immediately start getting better signal from customer conversations.

1.  **Talk about their life instead of your idea.** Don't pitch. Don't even mention your solution. Ask them how they currently solve the problem, what tools they use, what frustrates them. People are honest about their own life; they become liars the moment you ask them to speculate about a hypothetical future.
2.  **Ask about specifics in the past instead of generics or opinions about the future.** "Would you use this?" is a useless question. "When was the last time you actually did X?" is a great one. Past behaviour is the only honest predictor of future behaviour.
3.  **Talk less. Listen more.** Most founders use customer interviews as a disguised pitch. Stop. Your job is to shut up and absorb.

### A Concrete Example

Let's say you're building a project management tool for small agencies in Dhaka. Here's the wrong way to interview a potential customer:

> **You:** "I'm building a tool that uses AI to automatically prioritise tasks. Would you find that useful?"
> **Them:** "Oh yeah, that sounds really cool. We struggle with prioritisation all the time. I'd definitely use that."

Looks like validation. It isn't. You asked them to speculate about a hypothetical future. Of course they said yes — saying no feels rude.

Here's the Mom Test version:

> **You:** "Walk me through how you prioritised tasks last week."
> **Them:** "Honestly, we just use a WhatsApp group. The loudest person wins."
> **You:** "When was the last time a task fell through the cracks because of this?"
> **Them:** "Tuesday. We lost a client deliverable in the chat and had to apologise."
> **You:** "What did that cost you?"
> **Them:** "About 40,000 taka in revised scope, plus a lot of embarrassment."

Now you have signal. They have a real, recurring problem. They're already paying for it in lost money and reputation. And notice — you never even mentioned your product. You don't need to. The problem is real enough that when you eventually show them a solution, they'll pull it out of your hands.

### The Difference Between Stated and Revealed Requirements

This is where most product teams trip up. There are two kinds of requirements:

- **Stated requirements:** What the customer *says* they want. "We need a dashboard with charts." "We need an export to Excel button." "We need dark mode."
- **Revealed requirements:** What the customer's actual behaviour tells you they need. They need to make a decision every Friday and currently can't see the data in time. The "dashboard" is their proposed solution, not their requirement.

When a customer says "we need a dashboard", your job is not to go build a dashboard. Your job is to ask: "What decision are you trying to make on Friday that you can't make today?" Maybe the answer is a dashboard. Maybe it's a single SMS sent on Thursday night. Maybe it's an automated email with one number in it. You won't know until you dig.

This is the single most expensive mistake I see teams make — treating customer feature requests as requirements. They are not requirements. They are hypotheses, often poorly formed, about what might solve the underlying need.

### The Commitment Signal

Fitzpatrick introduces another gem: the difference between compliments and commitments. Compliments feel great in the moment and are worth nothing.

> "This is amazing, you should totally build this!"

Useless. Here's what matters:

- **Time:** "Can we schedule a follow-up next week to go deeper?"
- **Reputation:** "Would you introduce me to two other people who have this problem?"
- **Money:** "Would you pre-pay for three months at a 50% discount?"
- **Pilot access:** "Can we get access to your actual data for a two-week pilot?"

If they won't give you any of these, they don't have the problem badly enough. Move on. There are too many real problems in the world to waste time on lukewarm ones.

### Real Numbers From Real Failures

CB Insights analysed 156 startups that failed and asked the founders why. The number one reason, cited in 35% of cases, was "no market need". Not competition, not funding, not the team — building something nobody actually wanted. That's 55 startups that died because they believed customer compliments instead of customer behaviour.

The Mom Test is essentially a 150-page defence against this single failure mode. It's the cheapest insurance policy you'll ever read.

### How to Actually Run These Conversations

A few practical tips that took me painful trial and error to learn:

- **Never interview someone you have a sales relationship with.** They will tell you what you want to hear. Find neutral users, or people who don't even know you're building something.
- **Record the conversation** (with permission). You will misremember what was said. Notes lie. Recordings don't.
- **Write down the exact words they used.** "It's a nightmare" is different signal from "it's a minor inconvenience". Intensity matters.
- **Look for existing workarounds.** If someone has built a spreadsheet, a Notion doc, or a manual process to solve a problem, that problem is real and painful enough to pay for. If they have no workaround, the problem is theoretical.

### Conclusion: The Problem Is the Product

Most engineers, myself included, fall in love with solutions. We love the technology, the architecture, the elegant code. But the brutal truth of building products is this: the problem is the product. If you nail the problem, almost any reasonable solution will work. If you miss the problem, no amount of beautiful engineering will save you.

The Mom Test is a small, practical, brutally honest book. Read it before your next customer call. And then read it again after the call, to figure out which of your questions were still bad. The book is short — you can finish it in an afternoon. The conversations you run differently because of it will change what you build, and whether anyone cares when you ship it.
